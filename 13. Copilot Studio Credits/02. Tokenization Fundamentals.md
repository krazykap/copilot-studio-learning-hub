# 02. Tokenization Fundamentals
Tokenization is the process of breaking down text into smaller units called tokens. These tokens can be words, subwords, or even characters, depending on the tokenization method used. In the context of language models like GPT-3, tokenization is crucial for understanding and processing natural language input.

Before we can truly understand why one agent design costs more than another, we need to understand how tokenization works and how it affects the cost of using language models.

## What are Tokens?
A **token** is the smallest unit of text that a Large Language Model (LLM) processes.It is not a word, not a character, and not a sentence. It is something in between - and the exact boundaries are determined by the model's tokenizer: a pre-processing algorithm that splits raw text into these processing-ready chunks before the model ever sees it.

Think of tokenization as the LLM's version of reading. Just as humans process language word by word (and sometimes syllable by syllable for complex words), an LLM processes language token by token.

### How Tokenization Actually Works?
The most widely used tokenization approach in modern LLMs (including the GPT-family models that power Copilot Studio's Generative AI features) is called Byte Pair Encoding (BPE). Here is how it works conceptually:
- The tokenizer starts with a vocabulary of individual characters.
- It repeatedly merges the most frequently co-occurring character pairs into a single token.
- This process continues until the vocabulary reaches a target size (typically 50,000–100,000 tokens).
- The result is a vocabulary where common words are single tokens, uncommon words are split into sub-word tokens, and rare characters are represented individually.

## Token Boundaries: What Counts as One Token?
This is where things get interesting. Token boundaries do not align neatly with words or sentences. The rules are nuanced and depend on the specific tokenizer used by the model. The way text is tokenized can lead to surprising results in terms of how many tokens a given piece of text consumes. For example:
- The word "hello" might be tokenized as a single token.
- The phrase "hello world" might be tokenized as two tokens: "hello" and " world".
- The word "unhappiness" might be tokenized into three tokens: "un", "happiness", and a special end-of-word token.
- The sentence "I can't wait to use Copilot Studio!" might be tokenized into multiple tokens, including "I", "ca", "n't", "wait", "to", "use", "Copilot", "Studio", and "!".

This means that the same piece of text can consume a different number of tokens depending on how it is structured. For instance, "hello world" consumes two tokens, while "helloworld" might consume just one token. This is why the design of your agent's prompts and responses can significantly impact the number of tokens used, and therefore the cost.

**Common Words -> Single Tokens**

Frequently used words and phrases are often represented as single tokens in the model's vocabulary. This means that using common language can help reduce token count.

| Text | Approximate Token Count |
|------|-------------------------|
| "Hello" | 1 token |
| "the" | 1 token |
| "and" | 1 token |
| "Copilot" | 1 token |

**Uncommon Words -> Multiple Tokens**

Less common words, especially those that are longer or more complex, may be split into multiple tokens. This can increase the token count and therefore the cost.

| Text | Approximate Token Count |
|------|-------------------------|
| "unhappiness" | 3 tokens ("un", "happiness", end-of-word) |
| "antidisestablishmentarianism" | 5 tokens ("anti", "dis", "establish", "ment", "arianism") |

**Spaces, Punctuation, Numbers, and Special Characters -> Separate Tokens**

Spaces, punctuation marks, numbers, and special characters are often treated as separate tokens. This means that even a simple sentence can consume more tokens than expected due to these elements.

- **Spaces:** Each space between words is typically treated as a separate token. For example, "hello world" would consume two tokens: "hello" and " world".
- **Punctuation:** Punctuation marks are also treated as separate tokens. For example, "Hello, world!" would consume three tokens: "Hello", ",", and " world!".
- **Numbers:** Numbers are often treated as separate tokens. For example, "I have 2 dogs" would consume five tokens: "I", "have", "2", "dogs", and a space token.
- **Special Characters:** Special characters (like emojis or symbols) are also treated as separate tokens. For example, "I love 🍕!" would consume five tokens: "I", "love", "🍕", "!", and a space token.

**Non-English Languages -> Multiple Tokens**

This is especially important to keep in mind if your agent will be processing non-English text. Many languages, such as Chinese, Japanese, and Korean, do not use spaces to separate words. As a result, the tokenization process for these languages can lead to a much higher token count compared to English. For example, the Chinese sentence "我喜欢使用Copilot Studio" (I like using Copilot Studio) might be tokenized into multiple tokens for each character and word, leading to a higher overall token count.

| Language | Approximate Token Count |
|----------|-------------------------|
| English: "I like using Copilot Studio" | ~6 tokens ("I", "like", "using", "Copilot", "Studio") |
| Chinese: "我喜欢使用Copilot Studio" | ~10 tokens ("我", "喜欢", "使用", "Copilot", "Studio") |
| Japanese: "私はCopilot Studioが好きです" | ~12 tokens ("私", "は", "Copilot", "Studio", "が", "好き", "です") |

Understanding tokenization is essential for optimizing your agent's design and managing costs effectively. By being mindful of how your prompts and responses are structured, you can minimize unnecessary tokens and ensure that your agent operates efficiently within the constraints of the language model.

## Input Tokens vs Output Tokens
When calculating the cost of using a language model, it's important to distinguish between input tokens and output tokens. This is one of the most important distinctions in the entire topic. Not all tokens are equal - and understanding the difference between **input tokens** and **output tokens** is essential for understanding both cost and performance.

### Input Tokens (Prompt Tokens)
Input tokens are the tokens that you send to the model as part of your prompt. This includes all the text that you provide as input, such as instructions, context, and any user input. The number of input tokens directly affects the cost of the API call, as you are charged based on the total number of tokens processed by the model.
- **The System Prompt**: This is the initial instruction or context you provide to the model. It can be a few sentences or a detailed description of the task.
- **The Conversation History**: If your agent is designed to maintain context across multiple interactions, the conversation history will also contribute to the input token count. Each previous message in the conversation adds to the total number of tokens sent to the model.
- **The User's Current Input**: The latest message or query from the user is also part of the input tokens. Depending on the length and complexity of the user's input, this can significantly increase the token count.
- **Plugin Data**: If your agent uses plugins to fetch additional information or perform specific tasks, the data returned by these plugins can also contribute to the input token count when included in the prompt.
- **Adapative Cards**: If your agent uses adaptive cards to display information or gather user input, the content of these cards can also contribute to the input token count when included in the prompt.

Every single one of these elements contributes to the total number of input tokens, and therefore the cost of the API call. It's important to be mindful of how much information you include in the prompt, as excessive input tokens can quickly drive up costs.

### Output Tokens (Response Tokens)
Output tokens are the tokens generated by the model in response to your prompt. This includes the text that the model produces as its output, which can be a response to a user's query, a continuation of a conversation, or any other text generated based on the input. The number of output tokens also affects the cost, as you are charged for both input and output tokens. In Copilot Studio includes:
- The agent's response to the user, which can vary in length and complexity depending on the prompt and the model's capabilities.
- Structured JSON responses, which are often used for more complex interactions or when the agent needs to provide specific data or perform certain actions. These structured responses can also contribute to the output token count, especially if they include detailed information or multiple fields.
- Tool call instructions, which are generated by the model when it determines that a specific tool or plugin needs to be used to fulfill the user's request. These instructions can also contribute to the output token count, particularly if they are detailed or involve multiple steps.
- Reasoning traces, which are the internal thought processes of the model that may be included in the output for debugging or transparency purposes. These traces can also contribute to the output token count, especially if they are verbose or include detailed explanations.

### Why the Distinction Matters?
The distinction between input tokens and output tokens is crucial for understanding the cost structure of using language models. Both input and output tokens contribute to the total token count, which directly impacts the cost of API calls. By optimizing both the input and output token counts, you can manage costs more effectively while still providing a high-quality user experience. This matters for 3 reasons:
- **Output tokens cost more to generate than input tokens process -** This is because the model has to do more work to produce output tokens based on the input it receives. Therefore, it's important to be mindful of how much output you are generating, as excessive output can quickly drive up costs.
- **Output token length is partially within your control -** You can set limits on the maximum number of output tokens generated by the model, which can help manage costs. However, it's important to balance this with the need to provide a complete and useful response to the user.
- **Input tokens accumulate across a conversation -** If your agent maintains context across multiple interactions, the input token count can grow significantly over time as the conversation history expands. This can lead to higher costs if not managed properly.

               = ~500 input tokens

<b>Turn 2:</b>  [System Prompt] + [User Message 1] + [Agent Response 1] + [User Message 2]

Each turn processes an increasingly large input bundle. This is one of the most common sources of unexpectedly high credit consumption in production agents.

### Example A: Simple FAQ Interaction (Low Token, Low Credit)
**Agent Type:** IT Helpdesk bot with static knowledge base

**User Query:** "How do I reset my password?"

**Agent Response:** "To reset your password, please follow these steps: 1) Go to the Microsoft login page. 2) Click on 'Forgot Password'. 3) Enter your email address and submit. 4) Check your email for a password reset link and follow the instructions."

| Component | Approximate Tokens |
| --------- | ------------------ |
| System Prompt | ~130 tokens |
| User Query | ~10 tokens |
| Retrieved knowledge chunk (1 paragraph from SharePoint) | ~120 tokens |
| Agent Response | ~50 tokens |
| **Total Input Tokens** | **~310 tokens** |

**Assessment:** This interaction is relatively low in token count, resulting in a low credit cost. The agent is providing a straightforward response based on a single retrieved knowledge chunk, which keeps the input and output tokens manageable.

### Example B: Complex Policy Query (Medium Token, Medium Credit)
**Agent Type:** HR Policy bot with access to multiple documents
**User Query:** "I'm planning to take a sabbatical leave next year for 3 months to complete a professional certification. Can you walk me through the eligibility criteria, the application process, and what happens to my benefits during that period?"
**Agent Response:** Multi-paragraph response covering eligibility, process steps, and benefits implications.

| Component | Approximate Tokens |
| --------- | ------------------ |
| System Prompt (detailed HR persona, 300-word instruction set) | ~400 tokens |
| User Query | ~40 tokens |
| Retrieved knowledge chunks (3 policy sections from SharePoint) | ~600 tokens |
| Agent Response (3 paragraphs, detailed) | ~280 tokens |
| **Total Input Tokens** | **~1,320 tokens** |

**Assessment:** This interaction is more complex, with a higher token count due to the detailed system prompt, multiple retrieved knowledge chunks, and a longer agent response. The increased token count leads to a medium credit cost for this interaction.

### Example C: Multi-Turn Autonomous Agent (High Token, High Credit)
**Agent Type:** Procurement assistant with MCP tool access (Topics 11 & 12 patterns)
**Session:** 5-turn conversation where the agent retrieves supplier data, creates a purchase order draft, and seeks approval

| Turn | Input Tokens | Output Tokens | Notes |
| ---- | ------------ | ------------- | ----- |
| Turn 1 | ~800 tokens | ~120 tokens | Initial user request, system prompt and tool description |
| Turn 2 | ~1,400 tokens | ~200 tokens | Previous turn added to context + tool call result |
| Turn 3 | ~2,200 tokens | ~350 tokens | Previous turns + tool call result + Agent drafts PO, longer output |
| Turn 4 | ~3,000 tokens | ~100 tokens | User approves, short confirmation |
| Turn 5 | ~3,800 tokens | ~150 tokens | Agent finalizes PO and confirms completion |
| **Total** | **~11,200 input tokens** | **~900 output tokens** | **~12,100 total tokens. The cumulative effect of multiple turns and tool calls leads to a high token count and therefore a high credit cost.** |


**Assessment:** This multi-turn interaction demonstrates how token counts can grow significantly as the conversation progresses, especially when the agent maintains context across turns and utilizes tools that return additional information. The cumulative effect of input tokens from previous turns and the output tokens generated by the agent leads to a high total token count, resulting in a high credit cost for this session.

### Example D: The hiddne cost of verbose system prompts
Many makers underestimate the token cost of their system prompt, which is processed as input tokens on every single turn of the conversation. A verbose system prompt with detailed instructions, multiple examples, and a rich persona can easily consume 500+ tokens. If your agent has a long conversation with a user (10+ turns), that same system prompt could contribute 5,000+ input tokens across the session - even before you factor in the user's messages, retrieved knowledge, or the agent's responses. This is why it's crucial to optimize your system prompt for clarity and conciseness, ensuring it provides necessary guidance without being excessively verbose.
Consider 2 agents handling the same user query, but with different system prompt lengths:

| Component | Agent A (Lean Prompt) | Agent B (Verbose Prompt) |
| --------- | ------------------------ | ------------------------ |
| System Prompt | ~100 words | ~600 words |
| System Prompt Tokens | ~130 tokens | ~800 tokens |
| User message tokens | ~10 tokens | ~10 tokens |
| Knowledge retrieval tokens | ~120 tokens | ~120 tokens |
| Response tokens | ~50 tokens | ~50 tokens |
| **Total Tokens per turn** | **~260 tokens** | **~930 tokens** |
| **Multiplier** | **1x** | **~3.6x** |

If this agent handles 10,000 conversations per month, Agent B will consume approximately **3.6x** the credits of Agent A for doing the exact same job — simply because of a bloated system prompt.

## What Influences Credit Consumption (via tokenization)?
The number of tokens processed by the model directly influences the credit consumption of your agent. The more tokens you send as input and receive as output, the higher the cost. Therefore, understanding how tokenization works and how to optimize your prompts and responses can help you manage costs effectively while still providing a high-quality user experience. Key factors that influence credit consumption include:

| Factor | Token Impact | Credit Impact |
|-------|--------------|---------------|
| System Prompt Length | Longer prompts consume more input tokens on every turn, increasing costs across all interactions. | High |
| Response Length | Longer responses consume more output tokens, which are more expensive to generate than input tokens. | Medium to High |
| RAG Retrievals | Each retrieved knowledge chunk adds to the input token count, especially if multiple chunks are retrieved. | High |
| Conversation History | Maintaining context across multiple turns increases input tokens as the conversation history grows. | High |
| Tool Usage | Using tools that return additional information can increase both input and output tokens, depending on how the tool's output is integrated into the conversation. | Medium to High |
| Multiagent Interactions | If multiple agents are involved in a conversation, the token count can increase significantly due to the need to include context for each agent. | High |

## Bringing It Together: Tokens as the Foundation of Credit Literacy
Understanding tokenization is the foundation of credit literacy in Copilot Studio. By grasping how tokens work, how they are counted, and how they influence costs, you can make informed design decisions that optimize your agent's performance while managing credit consumption effectively. This knowledge allows you to strike the right balance between providing rich, informative interactions and keeping costs within your budget, ensuring that your agents can scale sustainably as they serve more users and handle more complex tasks.
